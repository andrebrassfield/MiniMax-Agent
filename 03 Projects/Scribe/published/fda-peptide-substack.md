# The FDA moved 7 peptides out of "definitely no." Here's what that actually changes for you.

The FDA has scheduled a Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting for July 23 and 24, 2026, at its White Oak Campus in Silver Spring, Maryland, to consider placing seven peptides on the 503A bulk drug substances list — the legal gateway to lawful compounding under Section 503A of the FD&C Act. That meeting is the next inflection point for any compounding pharmacy, telehealth clinic, or clinician currently treating patients with BPC-157, Semax, KPV, or any of the four other peptides on the agenda. Everything between now and then runs on a clock, and the clock has different speeds for different peptides.

## The 7, the 12, and the 13 — let's get the numbers straight

Before anything else, the numbers need to be untangled. Three different counts have been circulating, and conflating them is the most common way to misread the regulatory posture.

The **7** are the peptides on the PCAC's July 23–24 agenda. On July 23, the committee will consider: BPC-157 (free base / acetate), use evaluated: ulcerative colitis; KPV (free base / acetate), wound healing and inflammatory conditions; TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4, Fragment LKKTETQ), wound healing; and MOTs-C, obesity and osteoporosis. On July 24: Emideltide (also referred to as delta sleeping inducing peptide, DSIP), opioid withdrawal, chronic insomnia, and narcolepsy; Semax, cerebral ischemia, migraine, and trigeminal neuralgia; and Epitalon, insomnia. These seven are being considered for inclusion on the 503A bulks list — the legal pathway to compounding them under Section 503A.

The **12** are the peptides the FDA is removing from its Category 2 list — the "Do Not Compound" list — within seven calendar days of the April 15, 2026 notice. That is, by approximately April 22, 2026. The full enumeration: BPC-157; Cathelicidin LL-37; Dihexa Acetate; Emideltide (DSIP); Epitalon; GHK-Cu (for injectable routes of administration); KPV; Mechano Growth Factor, Pegylated (PEG-MGF); Melanotan II; MOTs-C; Semax (heptapeptide); and Thymosin Beta-4, Fragment (LKKTETQ) (TB-500).

The **13** adds one more: GHK-Cu (non-injectable) is being removed from Category 1, not Category 2. So the total number of peptides whose regulatory status just shifted is 13 — 12 from Category 2 and one from Category 1.

A few things to notice. First, seven of the 12 (BPC-157, Emideltide, Epitalon, KPV, MOTs-C, Semax, TB-500) appear on the July PCAC agenda. The remaining five — Cathelicidin LL-37, Dihexa Acetate, GHK-Cu (injectable), PEG-MGF, and Melanotan II — are not on the July agenda. Second, the FDA's action is driven by nomination withdrawal, not by an FDA safety reassessment. These peptides aren't being de-listed because the agency decided they were safe; they're being de-listed because the original nominators pulled their nominations. Third, the seven on the July agenda are being considered for inclusion on the 503A bulks list, not for re-placement in Category 1 — those are two different legal pathways with two different downstream effects.

## What's actually happening on July 23–24

The Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee will meet on Thursday, July 23, 2026 from 8:00 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. ET, and on Friday, July 24, 2026 from 8:00 a.m. to 3:50 p.m. ET. The location is the FDA White Oak Campus, 10903 New Hampshire Avenue, Building 31 Conference Center, the Great Room (Room 1503), Silver Spring, MD 20993-0002. Public participation is also available via online teleconferencing and video conferencing.

The committee's job, per the Federal Register notice (91 FR 20465, document 2026-07361, published April 16, 2026, signed by Grace R. Graham, Deputy Commissioner for Policy, Legislation, and International Affairs), is to discuss the seven bulk drug substances "being considered for inclusion on the 503A Bulks List." A public comment docket — Docket No. FDA-2025-N-6895 — is open until July 22, 2026, the day before the meeting. (One note: the FDA's meeting page contains a reference to a different docket number, FDA-2026-N-2979, in one of its instruction paragraphs. The Federal Register notice and the Regulations.gov docket both confirm FDA-2025-N-6895 as the comment-collection docket; the meeting-page reference appears to be a stale snippet or typo.)

The PCAC's role is advisory. The committee votes on whether to recommend inclusion; FDA then decides whether to accept, modify, or reject that recommendation. Even a favorable PCAC vote does not automatically place a substance on the 503A bulks list. FDA must still complete formal notice-and-comment rulemaking before any of these peptides can be lawfully compounded under Section 503A.

## What's the procedural timeline

Three dates matter. Approximately April 22, 2026 is the effective date for the Category 2 removal — by which point the 12 peptides are no longer in the "Do Not Compound" category. July 22, 2026 is the public-comment deadline for the PCAC meeting. July 23–24, 2026 is the meeting itself, where the seven peptides get a vote.

Two other things are happening in parallel. First, FDA has committed to scheduling a second PCAC meeting before the end of February 2027 to consider the remaining five peptides (Cathelicidin LL-37, Dihexa Acetate, GHK-Cu injectable, PEG-MGF, Melanotan II) — plus GHK-Cu non-injectable, which was previously in Category 1. The docket notice for that second meeting has not yet been published. Second, the PCAC currently has only four members, with several vacancies from members removed last year who have not been replaced. The committee does not presently appear to have a quorum, which is a procedural issue that could affect its ability to act at the scheduled July meeting. HHS Secretary Kennedy has authority to fill vacancies before then, and there are reports of forthcoming appointments, but as of the current dossier snapshot, the membership count is the binding number.

## BPC-157, KPV, Semax — the most-asked-about peptides

Three of the seven on the agenda are the peptides the practitioner community is asking about most. A short note on each, drawn from the Federal Register notice:

**BPC-157** is on the July 23 agenda, with ulcerative colitis as the use being evaluated. It is presented in the notice as the free base and as BPC-157 acetate.

**KPV** is also on July 23, with wound healing and inflammatory conditions as the uses being evaluated. It is presented in the notice as the free base and as KPV acetate.

**Semax** is on July 24, with cerebral ischemia, migraine, and trigeminal neuralgia as the uses being evaluated. The notice describes it as a heptapeptide and presents it as the free base and as Semax acetate.

The remaining four on the agenda — TB-500, MOTs-C, Emideltide/DSIP, and Epitalon — are less circulated in the practitioner community but each has a specific nominated therapeutic use, also drawn directly from the Federal Register notice. One clarifying note that matters: the "use evaluated" column in the notice lists the *uses FDA reviewed* for safety and effectiveness at the bulks-list evaluation stage, not approved indications and not on-label uses. A reader who confuses "use evaluated" with "approved indication" would overstate the regulatory effect of a future 503A listing.

## What's the enforcement picture

This is the part that matters most to a compounder deciding what to do on Monday morning. As of the current dossier snapshot, the 12 peptides removed from Category 2 exist in a regulatory gray area. They are not in Category 2 (the safety-risk designation). They are not in Category 1 (which would trigger enforcement discretion). They are not on the 503A bulks list (which would authorize compounding).

Per FDA's official 503A bulks page: "FDA does not intend to take action against a compounder for compounding drugs using bulk drug substances listed in category 1, provided that the conditions described in the guidance document are met." Category 2 substances are explicitly outside the scope of this interim enforcement-discretion policy. As Frier Levitt put it: "Removal from Category 2 does not render these bulk drug substances eligible for compounding under section 503A. They also remain outside the scope of the FDA's interim enforcement discretion policy, which currently extends only to substances in Category 1."

If the PCAC votes against inclusion for any of the seven, the substance could be placed in the "Other Bulk Drug Substances That May Present Significant Safety Risks" category — a separate bucket that would represent the most adverse possible outcome from a compoundability standpoint. If the PCAC votes in favor, FDA still has to complete formal rulemaking, and the actual compounding authorization comes only when a final rule is published. The interim enforcement-discretion extension — which would allow lawful compounding during the rulemaking process — is a separate FDA decision and is the lever compounders most care about. The dossier does not record any current FDA commitment to extend that discretion to any of the 12.

A point on the 2024 precedent: per Orrick, "the public advisory committee meetings convened in 2024 resulted in the PCAC voting against inclusion of all previous peptides under review, finding that they posed unacceptable safety risks for compounding." The Biden-era PCAC and the current PCAC are operating under materially different administrations, and the prior outcome is not a strong predictor of the current one. But the precedent is on the record.

## What to do before the meeting

Three concrete steps, all within what the sources support.

First, if your practice currently compounds any of the 12, treat the next seven weeks as a transition window. Compounding under 503A carries risk while these peptides are outside Category 1 and not on the bulks list; the regulatory exposure has not gone away, it has changed shape. If you are currently compounding GHK-Cu (non-injectable) — which is being removed from Category 1, a different bucket — that pathway is also unsettled until the second PCAC meeting.

Second, submit public comment to Docket No. FDA-2025-N-6895 before the July 22, 2026 deadline if you have clinical, manufacturing, or safety data relevant to any of the seven peptides. Comments go on the public docket and become part of what the committee sees. The current administration's posture on peptide access is more permissive than the prior administration's, but the PCAC's recommendation still needs to be supported by safety and effectiveness evidence on the record.

Third, secure pharmaceutical-grade API supply chains ahead of any favorable PCAC vote. Per Frier Levitt: "If the PCAC issues favorable recommendations and the FDA subsequently reclassifies any of these peptides into Category 1, the availability of pharmaceutical-grade API will likely become the principal bottleneck." Per Orrick: "To comply with section 503A, pharmacies will need to source pharmaceutical-grade API from FDA-registered manufacturers, accompanied by valid certificates of analysis; sourcing from unregulated suppliers is not permissible." Section 503A conditions are non-negotiable: the bulk drug substance must comply with a USP/NF monograph if one exists, be a component of an FDA-approved drug, or appear on the 503A bulks list; the manufacturer must be registered under Section 510; a valid certificate of analysis must accompany each batch; and compounding must be pursuant to a valid prescription for an individually identified patient.

## The question that should be answered on July 24

The procedural question — will the seven peptides be recommended for the 503A bulks list — is the headline outcome, but the more important question is whether FDA will, in parallel, extend its interim enforcement-discretion policy to cover any of the seven during the rulemaking process. The PCAC vote is the first of two gates. The second gate is the FDA's discretion to allow lawful compounding while the formal rulemaking plays out. The peptide community can live with a "no" on the PCAC vote if FDA has signaled an enforcement pathway; it cannot live with a "yes" on the PCAC vote and a "no" on enforcement discretion.

The other question — and the one with the higher stakes for the 5-peptide second meeting — is whether the PCAC will have a quorum on July 23. A meeting that cannot act is the worst-case procedural outcome: it does not produce a recommendation, it does not close the gray area, and it does not move any of the peptides toward lawful compounding. The 13-peptide family does not need a "no" vote to stay in regulatory limbo. It needs a "no vote possible" to stay in regulatory limbo, and that outcome is on the table if the vacancies are not filled.

After the meeting, watch the FDA's interim-policy page first. Watch the second PCAC meeting notice second. The compoundability clock resets only when one of those two updates lands.
