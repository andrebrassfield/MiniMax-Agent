# Dossier — FDA Section 503A Bulk Drug Substances: April 16, 2026 Federal Register Notice & July 23–24, 2026 PCAC Meeting (Peptide Compounding)

> Living topic file. Captures the FDA's April 15/16, 2026 actions on peptide compounding under section 503A of the FD&C Act: the 12-peptide Category 2 removal, the July 23–24, 2026 PCAC meeting agenda for 7 of those peptides, the second PCAC meeting before end of February 2027 for the remaining 5, and the procedural status compounders face in the interim.

## Why this topic matters to Andre

Peptide compounding is the most active regulatory action in pharmacy law in mid-2026. The FDA removed 12 peptides from the "Do Not Compound" Category 2 list, scheduled a PCAC meeting to consider placing 7 of them on the 503A bulks list (the legal gateway to compounding), and committed to a second PCAC meeting before end of February 2027 for the remaining 5. The shape of the agenda — which peptides, which uses, which day — directly affects whether telehealth platforms, compounding pharmacies, and clinical practices can lawfully compound these substances. The regulatory gray area in between is also a major commercial and legal risk: removal from Category 2 is *not* placement on the 503A bulks list, and is *not* the same as receiving enforcement discretion. Andre's interest is twofold: (1) the regulatory mechanics (PCAC votes, formal rulemaking, the 7-day effective date, the 503A conditions), and (2) the upstream driver (HHS Secretary Kennedy's February 2026 Rogan-appearance announcement, signaling the administration's intent to expand access through lawful compounding channels). The dossier must be defensible line-by-line because the Scribe's zero-hallucination constraint means any fact here is the universe downstream agents can use.

## Implications for legal compounding under 503A (within what the sources say — not extrapolated)

**As of 2026-06-05, the 12 peptides removed from Category 2 exist in a regulatory gray area. Compounding them under 503A carries risk; not compounding them may also be a lost commercial opportunity if FDA later places them on the 503A bulks list. The dossier does not recommend a course of action — it captures what the sources say. The implications below are directly traceable to the cited primary and secondary sources.**

1. **What compounders can do today (post-April 22, 2026 effective date, pre-July 23 PCAC).** Compounding the 12 peptides is *not* authorized under FDA's interim enforcement policy — the policy applies only to Category 1 substances. Per Frier Levitt: "Removal from Category 2 does not render these bulk drug substances eligible for compounding under section 503A. They also remain outside the scope of the FDA's interim enforcement discretion policy, which currently extends only to substances in Category 1." Per Orrick: "compounding these substances carries regulatory risk." (clm-2026-06-05-006, clm-2026-06-05-007)

2. **What the 503A conditions require (always, regardless of any future listing).** Per FDA's official 503A page: bulk drug substance must (a) comply with USP/NF monograph if one exists, (b) be a component of an FDA-approved drug if monograph does not exist, OR (c) appear on the 503A bulks list. Plus: must be accompanied by a valid certificate of analysis, manufactured at an FDA-registered facility (section 510), and compounded pursuant to a valid prescription for an individually identified patient. (clm-2026-06-05-010)

3. **What happens if the PCAC recommends inclusion at the July 23–24 meeting.** Per Frier Levitt: "If the PCAC issues favorable recommendations and the FDA subsequently reclassifies any of these peptides into Category 1, the availability of pharmaceutical-grade API will likely become the principal bottleneck." Per Orrick: "pharmacies holding state licenses and meeting all other 503A conditions would be authorized to prepare patient-specific formulations of the included peptides — a substantial commercial opportunity. To comply with section 503A, pharmacies will need to source pharmaceutical-grade API from FDA-registered manufacturers, accompanied by valid certificates of analysis; sourcing from unregulated suppliers is not permissible." (clm-2026-06-05-008 → implications)

4. **What happens if the PCAC votes against inclusion.** Per Orrick: "the substances could be placed in the 'Other Bulk Drug Substances That May Present Significant Safety Risks' category." PCAC's recommendations are advisory, and FDA retains authority to accept, modify, or reject them. (clm-2026-06-05-008)

5. **Procedural gatekeeping.** The PCAC vote is the first of two steps. Even a favorable PCAC vote does not automatically place a substance on the 503A bulks list. FDA must still complete formal rulemaking (notice-and-comment). The interim enforcement-discretion extension is a separate FDA decision and is the lever compounders most care about. (clm-2026-06-05-006, clm-2026-06-05-007)

6. **PCAC quorum watch-item.** Frier Levitt flags: PCAC "does not presently appear to have a quorum" — only 4 members, several vacancies. If the vacancies are not filled by July 23, the committee may be unable to act on the 7 peptides on its agenda. (clm-2026-06-05-011) — **Watch-item, not a blocker; Secretary Kennedy has authority to fill vacancies.**

7. **2024 precedent.** Per Orrick: "the public advisory committee meetings convened in 2024 resulted in the PCAC voting against inclusion of all previous peptides under review, finding that they posed unacceptable safety risks for compounding." The Biden-era precedent and the current administration's posture are materially different, so the prior outcome is not a strong predictor. (clm-2026-06-05-011)
