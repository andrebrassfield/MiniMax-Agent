# 15-Properties Quality Rubric for Atomic Notes

> Christopher Alexander spent 20 years studying what makes a building, a place, a process, or a structure **feel alive** — what he called "the Quality Without a Name" (WQN). In *The Nature of Order* (2002-2004, four volumes) he identified **15 structural properties** that reoccur in every structure with high WQN. The properties are not aesthetic preferences; they are empirical regularities. They apply not just to architecture but to anything with structure: arguments, organizations, code, notes. This pattern uses Alexander's 15 properties as a **quality rubric for CHIEF atomic notes** — a way to score, compare, and repair notes for "wholeness."

## The 15 properties (canonical list, from Alexander Vol 1, pp. 141-242)

1. **Levels of Scale** — entities of different sizes step in small jumps; no large scale gaps.
2. **Strong Centers** — every part has a clearly identifiable focus; the structure has gravity.
3. **Thick Boundaries** — interfaces are *zones*, not walls; they support exchange.
4. **Alternating Repetition** — two repeating systems of centers embellish each other.
5. **Positive Space** — the shape of the void is as intentional as the shape of the solid.
6. **Good Shape** — the part has an internal coherence that is locally recognizable.
7. **Local Symmetries** — symmetry appears at multiple sub-scales.
8. **Deep Interlock and Ambiguity** — parts belong to multiple larger wholes simultaneously.
9. **Contrast** — each part is strengthened by being different from its neighbors.
10. **Gradients** — qualities change smoothly across the structure, not in jumps.
11. **Roughness** — slight irregularity at the small scale, not mechanical perfection.
12. **Echoes** — the same idea appears at different scales, with variations.
13. **The Void** — the structure has a calming, expansive, unfilled center.
14. **Simplicity and Inner Calm** — no unnecessary elements; the structure is at peace.
15. **Not Separateness** — the structure feels connected to its context, not extracted from it.

Alexander's claim: **15 is an empirical count, not a logical necessity.** It could be 10 or 20. But the cluster is real.

## The principle

A CHIEF atomic note is a structured object. It has a frontmatter, a lead quote, sections, examples, connections, and a closing. **The question "is this note alive?" is not subjective** — Alexander's 15 properties give an operational way to ask it. The rubric turns "wholeness" from a vibe into a property you can check.

The pattern: **score every atomic note on 15 dimensions.** Each property is 0-2 (absent / present-but-weak / strongly-present). Total score = 0-30. Notes below 18 need structure surgery; notes 18-24 are alive; notes 24+ are exemplary.

## The mechanism (how to apply it)

For each property, ask the note one question:

| # | Property | Question to ask the note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Levels of Scale | Does the note nest smaller ideas inside larger ones at consistent ratios? |
| 2 | Strong Centers | Is there one clear central claim, even if the note has many sections? |
| 3 | Thick Boundaries | Are the sections of the note connected by transitional zones, not abrupt jumps? |
| 4 | Alternating Repetition | Do examples and principles alternate in a way that reinforces both? |
| 5 | Positive Space | Is the structure of the note (its sections, its rhythm) doing real work, or is it decoration? |
| 6 | Good Shape | Can you recognize this note by its silhouette (its section headers) from across the room? |
| 7 | Local Symmetries | Are parallel sections (e.g., "What this is NOT" / "What would falsify") mirror each other? |
| 8 | Deep Interlock | Does each section belong to the whole AND contribute to a larger context (MOC, parent note)? |
| 9 | Contrast | Are different sections genuinely different in voice, length, or stance — or are they the same? |
| 10 | Gradients | Does the note's energy/intensity change smoothly (e.g., thesis → evidence → caveat → connection)? |
| 11 | Roughness | Does the note admit the occasional quirk, the honest aside, the imperfect example? |
| 12 | Echoes | Does a key term or motif appear 2-3 times in different sections, with variations? |
| 13 | The Void | Does the note have a moment of breathing room — a closing that opens, not a closing that closes? |
| 14 | Simplicity and Inner Calm | Could you cut 20% of the note without losing the message? If not, the note is loud, not calm. |
| 15 | Not Separateness | Does the note end with a connection to a larger structure (a MOC, a connection note, the next note)? |

## The deeper math (for the curious)

- The 15 properties are not a checklist — they are **correlated**. A note with strong Strong Centers tends to also have Good Shape; a note with broken Levels of Scale tends to also have broken Gradients.
- The 15 properties **emerge from a process**, not a static state. Alexander's Vol 2 identifies 10 structure-enhancing actions that, when applied step-by-step, produce a structure that exhibits the 15 properties. The rubric measures the *result* of the process, not the process itself.
- The properties are **scale-invariant.** A sentence can have Good Shape. A book can have Good Shape. The 15 properties apply at every level of the structure.

## Isomorphism to Omni-Operator

The pattern operationalizes the question: **"Is this note alive, or is it well-formatted dead prose?"** The 15 properties distinguish:

- A note that is **well-structured but inert** (high in Levels of Scale, low in Deep Interlock and Echoes) — likely a reference, not a thinking tool.
- A note that is **alive but messy** (high in Roughness, Alternating Repetition, Deep Interlock — low in Gradients) — likely a working draft.
- A note that is **alive AND well-structured** (high in most properties, including Simplicity) — likely an exemplar.

The pattern also says **don't delete weak notes; repair them.** Alexander's process is incremental, surgical, structure-preserving. You don't replace a weak note with a strong one — you enhance it, step by step, to make its existing centers more alive.

## What this is NOT

- **Not a style guide.** The 15 properties don't dictate voice, prose quality, or topic. They are structural.
- **Not a substitute for judgment.** A note can score high on all 15 and still be wrong. The rubric measures *wholeness*, not *truth.*
- **Not exhaustive.** Alexander admits the 15 are the empirical cluster, not the logical one. We may add a 16th, 17th as the vault grows. The 15 is the current best.
- **Not gate-keeping.** A note that scores 12/30 is not "bad." It's a working note. The rubric is for distinguishing working notes from dead ones, not for blocking.

## What would falsify

- The 15 properties are architectural, not intellectual. They don't transfer. **Counter: Alexander's own work shows they transfer to non-geometric domains (organizations, education, software). Notes are not pure geometry, but they are structured.**
- The rubric produces a score that doesn't correlate with the reader's experience of "this note helped me." **Counter: the rubric is a first pass; iterate based on feedback. Start with a clear property list and tune.**
- Applying the rubric to every note is too slow. **Counter: the rubric is for evaluation, not for first draft. Write first, evaluate at link-time. Most notes need <5 minutes of evaluation.**
